Start Here: What I Believe About Energy

Before the numbers, the philosophy. This is the lens I run my company through — agree or disagree, at least you'll know where I stand.

Our civilization has three looming issues: water, housing, and energy. Energy is everything — how we move, how we heat and cool our homes, how we power our industries and our technology. Right now over 60% of US power comes from fossil fuels, and every time we burn them, we lose an energy source instantly and permanently. There is no recycling a barrel of oil.

Solar is different in two ways that matter. First, it's predictable — the sun comes up every single day, and panels last 25-30+ years. Second, it's circular — up to 95% of a panel's materials can be recycled into new panels. Like a Diet Coke can going back on the shelf. Every system we install buys our civilization time to develop even better solutions: longer-lasting batteries, more powerful panels, small modular reactors. That's why I left a chemical engineering career in oil and gas for this. I saw both sides. I picked the one that thinks forward.

Honestly? Both. Here's the truth: having solar changes almost nothing about your daily life. Done right, you'll forget it's on your roof — it just provides power like your utility does now. Most sales reps are right about that part.

But how you go solar — the program you sign, who does the construction, how the system is sized, and what equipment goes up — has a massive impact on whether solar is a blessing or a nightmare. The gap between a great solar experience and a horror story isn't the technology. It's the decisions made before install day. That's what this entire knowledge center exists to fix.

Solar isn't perfect, and anyone who tells you it is should worry you. The real problems: storage is still the big one — the sun doesn't shine at night, and batteries are the most expensive, fastest-evolving part of any system. Incorrect disposal — panels ending up in landfills instead of recyclers. Trust — the industry has earned its reputation for shady sales tactics, hidden escalators, and companies that vanish. And transparency in offerings — most homeowners never learn what a fair price looks like.

Every one of these is solvable, and most are being solved right now. But you deserve to know they exist. Solar is not THE solution to our energy future — it's A solution, and a genuinely great one when done honestly.

Solar tech dates to the 1960s; SunPower was founded in 1985; manufacturing scaled through the 2000s as net metering and the federal tax credit made rooftop solar mainstream. SolarCity, Vivint, and Sunrun built the modern sales model. Then the turbulence: California ended NEM 1.0, then NEM 2.0 in 2023 — a 75% cut to export credits. Interest rates spiked. Financiers delayed payments to installers. SunPower, Titan Solar, Lumio, and many others went bankrupt.

I lived this personally — the company I loved working with, KOTA, did things right and still collapsed in 2024 because the market storm took good companies along with bad. The lesson isn't "avoid solar." It's structure your deal so a company's death can't hurt you — manufacturer warranties, insured agreements, and financially stable backers. That thinking runs through every recommendation on this site.

The Utility & The Grid

Why your bill keeps climbing, where the grid is heading, and the structural math of why solar wins.

A few compounding forces. The grid is old — much of it built decades ago and now incurring enormous maintenance costs. Wildfire liability — utilities pay billions in settlements and grid-hardening, and ratepayers foot the bill. Transmission spending — and here's the structural problem: California utilities earn a regulated profit margin on infrastructure spending. The more they spend, the more they earn. That's a system that rewards building, not saving.

The result: SDG&E rates average around $0.40-0.45/kWh — among the very highest in America — and 5-8% annual increases have been the norm, with some years hitting 40%+ cumulative over just a few years. An average San Diego home using 700 kWh/month pays over $300 for power that would cost $112 in Texas. Same electrons. Very different bill.

Because it's not a fair fight — it's structural. Your utility bill is rent with infinite interest: a payment that never ends, increases 5-8% every year, and leaves you owning nothing. A $250/month bill today becomes $164,000+ paid over 25 years at typical escalation — and on day 9,125 you own exactly as much of the grid as you did on day one. Nothing.

Every solar path redirects that same budget you're already spending toward something better: ownership paths build equity and eventually end; a PPA locks a rate 30-50% below the utility with zero maintenance. Even the "worst" solar option beats the utility long-term when priced fairly. The utility's only advantages are familiarity and doing nothing. Those are expensive advantages.

Compare all five paths side-by-side →

Net Metering (NEM) — still the rule in most states — lets you sell excess solar to the grid at retail rate. Produce more than you use across the year and your bill hits ~$0. Simple, forgiving, oversizing was smart.

Net Billing — California since 2023 — cut export credits by ~75%. Selling to the grid is now a bad trade for most hours of the year, which means the winning design changed completely: size solar to your daytime usage, add a battery to store your own power for nighttime, and minimize grid interaction. The twist: during August-September peak hours, SDG&E pays $1-$3.50/kWh for exports — so a well-designed battery system cashes in exactly when the grid is desperate. This is why systems designed after 2023 must be designed with software and your actual usage data (Green Button data), not napkin math. It's also why so many post-2023 systems from careless companies underperform.

Most California utilities now charge based on when you use power, not just how much. Power is most expensive 4-9pm — when everyone's home — often 2-4x the midday rate. This has literally changed how people live: laundry after midnight, AC off on sweltering evenings, pool pumps running at 2am even though they work better in daylight.

Solar + battery flips this completely. Your panels make power when it's nearly free (daytime), your battery deploys it when the utility charges the most (evening). Instead of arranging your life around rates, the system arranges the rates around your life. That freedom — using power when you actually want to — is one of the most underrated things solar buys you.

Community Choice Aggregators are locally-controlled, not-for-profit power providers. SDG&E still owns the wires and sends the bill, but the CCA buys the actual electricity — typically cleaner and at competitive rates, with profits reinvested locally instead of paid to shareholders. In our area: San Diego Community Power (SDCP) serves much of the region, and Clean Energy Alliance (CEA) serves 255,000+ customers in Carlsbad, Del Mar, Escondido, Solana Beach, San Marcos, Oceanside, and Vista.

Why homeowners should care: CCAs run programs the big utility never would — SDCP's battery rebates ($3,000-$6,000 per battery) and CEA's Solar Plus ($0-down, no-credit-check solar and batteries). Being in CCA territory is an advantage. Use it.

Decentralized. The old model — giant power plants pushing electricity one direction over long wires — is aging out at the exact moment demand is exploding (data centers, EVs, electrification of everything). The new model is millions of small power plants: rooftops and garages, aggregated into Virtual Power Plants that respond to the grid's needs in real time.

This isn't sci-fi — it's already paying homeowners. VPP programs pay yearly rebates for letting your battery support the grid during peak events. Utilities are crediting $1-$3.50/kWh for peak-hour exports. Every year the grid gets more desperate for local storage, your battery gets more valuable. Homeowners who install solar + storage now aren't just cutting a bill — they're buying a seat at the table of the next energy economy.

The tax credit changed everything in 2026. Here's the short version — and the deep dive when you're ready.

Really. The residential credit (Section 25D) expired December 31, 2025. Buy a system with cash or a loan in 2026 and there's no federal credit for you — full stop. What survived is the commercial credit (Section 48E), alive through roughly end of 2027, and it belongs to whoever owns the system.

So the play now: programs where a company owns your system (PPA, prepaid ESA, lease), claims the ~30% credit, and passes the value through as a lower price. Add up to 10% more in energy community zip codes. This is why solar prices in 2026 are no higher than before — despite pricier materials. But it has an expiration date. Prepaid ESA (like Propel): a company owns your system ~5 years, claims the commercial credit, gives you 30-40% off, then ownership transfers to you. Discount + equity. My general favorite for qualifying credit.

PPA: you buy the power, not the panels — $0.17-$0.27/kWh vs SDG&E's $0.40+, with all maintenance, repairs, and replacements covered for 25 years. Watch the escalator (0-3.9%/year), and know that "fixed payment" sales language often hides one.

Cash/loan: cleanest ownership, but with no federal credit anymore, the math flipped hard. Only pencils if outright day-one ownership matters more to you than total savings.

Every PPA has a yearly escalator — the percent your payment increases annually, anywhere from 0% to 3.9%. Because the payment is the same every month, reps love calling it "fixed" while glossing over the yearly climb. "It goes up no more than 3.9%" means it goes up 3.9% every year.

An escalator isn't inherently bad — a higher escalator buys you the biggest day-one savings of any solar option, and it's still better than the utility's trajectory. But hiding it is disqualifying. Fair San Diego pricing with battery: $0.20-$0.24/kWh at 3.5% escalation, or $0.28-$0.31/kWh at 0%. Dramatically lower quotes usually mean the battery payment is hiding on a separate line. Do the math yourself: total monthly payment ÷ average monthly production = your real $/kWh.

Not a scam — a trade-off most reps never explain. Real solar loan interest runs 6-12%. Financiers offer 3-5% rates by "buying down the rate" with an upfront dealer fee baked into your project price — sometimes 25-35% of the total.

The sliding scale: staying 10+ years and never paying early? Low rate + high fee wins. Paying off fast or moving soon? No fee + higher rate wins. In the middle? A 10-15% fee splits the difference. The scam isn't the fee — it's signing up for a huge fee without knowing it exists, then paying off a loan early and eating a fee you never needed. Ask for both quotes side-by-side. A good rep shows you the comparison without being asked.

Almost every modern agreement transfers to the buyer — who inherits a power rate locked below the utility, which good real estate agents position as a selling point. Owned systems are cleaner still: they simply add home value ($15-25K typically).

Two cautions: PPAs carry prepayment penalties before year 5, so if you're selling within 5 years, make sure your agent knows how to transfer payments — or choose a program with flexible buyouts. And Sunrun-style providers only offer buyouts at certain years, which limits your options at closing. Tell your rep your realistic timeline in your home and let it shape the program choice. It matters more than people think.

Rebates & Incentives

The layers stack — and several are running out of money in real time. Current as of mid-2026.

San Diego Community Power's battery program pays $3,000-$6,000 per battery depending on your qualifications — and it applies whether you're pairing a battery with brand-new solar or adding one to an existing system.

The urgency is real: as of mid-2026, roughly 30-40% of program funds remain, and once they're reserved, they're gone. This is a first-come program, not a permanent entitlement. Verify current status at sdcommunitypower.org — and if you've been on the fence about a battery, this rebate is the fence-remover.

If you live in Carlsbad, Del Mar, Escondido, Solana Beach, San Marcos, Oceanside, or Vista, Clean Energy Alliance's Solar Plus program is one of the most accessible offers in the country: solar + battery (or battery-only) with zero upfront cost and no credit check. Administered by CEA with Participate.Energy; Tesla designs, equips, and services the systems; payments land right on your utility bill; and all equipment replacements are covered for the minimum 25-year term.

The sleeper play I keep telling people about: battery-only for $0 down. If you already have solar, a battery lets you stop selling cheap at noon and buying expensive at 7pm — and maximize those nighttime sell-back credits. CEA also runs income-qualified programs (Battery Bonus Connect and Solar Plus Connect, funded through SGIP). Details: thecleanenergyalliance.org/solar-plus.

Certain zip codes qualify as "energy communities" under federal rules — areas historically tied to fossil fuel employment or meeting other criteria. Systems installed there earn an extra adder on top of the ~30% commercial credit, pushing programs like Propel from 30% off to 39.2% off.

You do nothing to earn this except live where you live. But it changes which program wins for your house — in an energy community zip, prepaid ESA programs get dramatically stronger. It's the first thing I check when running your numbers, and it's why the same system can be priced differently one neighborhood over.

For providers to claim the commercial credit on your system, a required percentage of the equipment must be made from domestic materials. This is why programs specify hardware — Propel requires Enphase gear partly because it satisfies domestic content rules.

The homeowner consequence: qualifying American-made materials are in limited supply against surging demand. That's the quiet clock ticking behind 2026-2027 pricing — when the qualifying materials run out, the discounts tied to them end. It's also, honestly, good for the country: this rule has pulled panel and component manufacturing back onshore at record pace.

Sacramento-area homeowners: SMUD runs battery rebates that stack with financing programs — check SMUD's site for current amounts and terms.

Bigger picture, VPP programs are rolling out everywhere, and they change battery math permanently: instead of a one-time rebate, they pay you per year for letting your battery support the grid during peak events. Every year enrolled shrinks your battery's real cost. As utilities get more desperate for local storage — and they will — expect these yearly payments to grow. A battery in 2026 isn't just backup; it's a small income-producing asset.

Net billing slashed export credits for most of the year — but during 1-2 hours a day in August and September, SDG&E pays $1 to $3.50 per kWh for power sent to the grid. That's up to 8x their own retail rate.

A well-designed solar + battery system charges itself on nearly-free sunshine all year, then cashes in during exactly those desperate late-summer peak hours. These credits are a major reason the best true-up date is July 1st (right before peak sun), and why proper system design in the net billing era can still get you to a ~$0 monthly bill. It takes software, your Green Button usage data, and a designer who actually cares. But the window is there.

Batteries & Storage

The most critical — and most misunderstood — piece of a modern California system.

Under net billing — yes, for almost everyone. Export credits were cut ~75%, so selling your excess solar to the grid is a losing trade most of the year. A battery stores your own daytime power for your own nighttime use, which is now the whole game. Without one, you'd need to size solar to only ~60-70% of usage and accept a permanent utility bill.

Batteries also unlock the stackable money: SDCP's $3,000-$6,000 rebate, VPP yearly payments, and those $1-$3.50/kWh August-September export credits. In 2026 California, the battery isn't the add-on. It's the strategy.

Usually not — and some batteries provide no backup at all, by design. Three configurations:

  • Grid-tied (no backup): replaces the grid at night for maximum savings, zero backup capability. Cheapest, and perfect if outages aren't your concern.
  • Partial backup: the most common. Extra equipment backs up 2-10 essential circuits — fridge, lights, wifi. In a blackout, only those run.
  • Whole-home backup: multiple batteries, most expensive, everything runs — until the batteries drain, so conservation still matters.

Modern systems "island" during outages: your solar recharges your batteries by day even while the grid is down. But sizing backup honestly — instead of letting a rep imply one battery runs your AC through a week-long outage — is the difference between a happy customer and a furious one.

Capacity (kWh) is how far a car can drive — the total energy stored. Power (kW) is the speed limit — how fast energy can flow out. A big battery with low power is a truck with a huge gas tank and a 25mph governor: lots of range, can't run the heavy stuff.

Heavy appliances — AC, pool pumps, EV chargers, dryers, heat pumps — need high power (generally 5kW+) just to switch on. They also drain capacity fast. This is why battery specs matter more than brand names: a Tesla Powerwall 3 (13.5kWh / 11.5kW) and an Enphase 5P (5kWh / 3.85kW) are built for completely different jobs, and both are excellent at their own job. Batteries stack like batteries in a remote — two 10kWh units = 20kWh — so systems are built from the load up.

Most batteries carry 10-year warranties; Enphase and Franklin stand out at 15. Nearly all guarantee ≥70% capacity at end of warranty. But the fine print has two traps:

Cycles: like a car's "5 years or 50,000 miles," some warranties cap full charge/discharge cycles (4,000-15,000). Daily-use batteries burn cycles fast — if you're cycling nightly (which is the whole point under net billing), a higher cycle limit or unlimited-cycle warranty matters enormously. Throughput: some cap total lifetime energy (e.g., 60MWh). Exceed either limit and the warranty is void even inside the time window. Backup-only batteries can ignore this; nightly-cycling batteries can't. This single paragraph has saved my clients more money than most sales pitches.

I'll be straight with you: I don't publish battery pricing anymore, because every home is genuinely different — backup needs, panel compatibility, electrical configuration, and which rebates you qualify for swing the number by thousands. Ballpark for battery-only projects after incentives: $15,000-$18,000 per battery, but the SDCP rebate, CEA's $0-down program, and VPP payments can transform that math completely.

What I'll say with confidence: battery pricing is only going up. Materials are constrained, demand is exploding, and every VPP program launched makes storage more valuable. Adding a battery retroactively also always costs more than including it with solar. If a battery is in your future, the cheapest day to do it is today.

System Design & Equipment

The most critical component of any system isn't a component — it's the design. Get this right and everything else follows.

Because nothing survives a bad design. Sweet deal, cheap price, best equipment ever — none of it matters if the system is sized wrong. You'll have a confusing, frustrating, and long-term costly experience anyway.

A proper design accounts for four things: (1) your current usage, (2) your utility's metering program, (3) when you use power, and (4) what you're adding in the next 18 months — EV, hot tub, heat pump, more kids. Under net billing, this requires your utility's Green Button data — 15-minute interval usage — fed into design software that models sun data against your actual life. If a rep sizes your system off one monthly bill total, they're guessing. Guessing is how people end up with surprise true-up bills.

My favorite question. Reality: the US imports 88% of its panels and less than 1% come from China — China makes components, but assembly happens elsewhere (and domestic content rules are pulling manufacturing to the US fast).

What actually matters: power (most panels are 400-500W now; higher wattage only matters with limited roof space), efficiency (18-23% — paying premium prices for 18% panels is what we call highway robbery), and longevity (the performance warranty: good = 86% output at 25 years; premium = 92%). Rough quality ladder: Maxeon > REC > Canadian > Silfab > Qcells > Panasonic > Jinko > Longi > Trina. Moving soon? Cheap panels fine. Forever home? REC or better. Best value balance? Silfab or Qcells — negligible performance difference, real savings.

The inverter converts your panels' DC power to usable AC. String inverters (SolarEdge, Tesla) run sets of panels through one box: more efficient and cheaper, but one shaded panel drags down its whole string, and if the inverter dies, the whole system is down. Tesla's is 12.5-year warrantied with famously thin customer service; SolarEdge has known failure rates that make many installers wary.

Microinverters (Enphase, essentially alone here) put one on every panel: one fails, the rest keep producing. 25-year warranties, excellent support, the best app ecosystem, and the natural pairing for Enphase storage. My rule: beautiful sunny roof + cost focus = string. Shade, long-term security, or future Enphase battery plans = micros, no contest.

If your home is 15+ years old, your electrical panel may cap your solar before you start. Utilities limit "backfeed" by your panel's rating — the formula: (120% × service rating) − main breaker = allowable backfeed, and max system size ≈ a quarter of that in kW. A typical old 100A/100A panel allows only a 4-5kW system. Upgrading to 200A typically runs $3,000-$4,000 — cheap if your service comes in overhead, potentially $12,000+ if underground trenching is required.

Escape hatches exist: 125A upgrades without trenching, renewable meter adapters (RMA) that feed solar behind the meter, and batteries that circumvent backfeed limits. Some old panels (Zinsco, Sylvania, Stab-Lok) are fire hazards that must be replaced regardless. Here's the tell: if your home is older and a rep never mentions your electrical panel, that's a red flag the size of a billboard. Included with solar, the upgrade rides your financing.

The industry's real pricing language is Price Per Watt: total cost ÷ total watts installed. A 6kW system quoted at $21,000 = $3.50/W. That one number lets you compare any two quotes regardless of panel count or sales theater.

Fair 2026 cash pricing for the solar portion (California, before battery/MPU/extras): roughly $2.55-$2.80/W for budget equipment, $2.70-$3.05/W mid-tier, $2.85-$3.30/W premium with microinverters. To compare honestly, strip out the extras first: subtract the battery, panel upgrade, and any dealer fee from the total, THEN divide by watts. And remember — the lowest price is not the best price. Companies need margin to still exist when your warranty call comes.

EV chargers run $500-$1,500 installed depending on the electrical work. Bigger breaker = faster charging = more panel capacity required. Plug-in hybrid? A 30-40A charger is plenty (or just use the dryer outlet). Full EV? Go 50-70A — you'll want the speed.

Two smart moves: bundle the charger with your solar install so it rides your financing and program benefits, and tell your designer about the EV even if it's 18 months away — charging at night means more battery; charging midday means more solar. An EV can double a home's electricity usage. Surprise true-up bills love an unplanned EV.

Repairs, Service & Audits

What actually goes wrong, what maintenance really looks like, and how to know if your system is earning what it should.

Shockingly little — this is one place the sales guys undersell. Panels have no moving parts and run 25-30+ years. The honest maintenance list: watch your monitoring app (5 minutes a month), clean the panels once a year in low-rain areas like ours (before summer, after pollen season — de-ionized water and regular soap, never when panels are hot, or just hire a cleaner), and a system health check every 5-10 years.

The components that can actually need service over decades: inverters (that's why warranty length matters — 12.5 vs 25 years is a real difference) and batteries (10-15 year warranties). The panels themselves? They'll likely outlive your roof.

Your app (Enphase, Tesla, SolarEdge, or your PPA provider's) shows production and, with a battery, storage behavior. The one metric that matters: production versus the same period last year. Weather varies, seasons swing — but if production drops significantly in a short window or year-over-year with similar weather, a panel or inverter likely needs service. That's genuinely the only failure mode solar-only systems really have.

Battery acting weird? Check the mode first — standby/backup (saves for emergencies), self-consumption (drains nightly), and time-of-use (follows rate schedules) behave completely differently, and a mode switch is the #1 "my battery is broken" false alarm. Not charging fully or dumping too fast on the right mode? Something's pulling more power than it can serve, or it needs service. Then you call — and if your installer is gone, that's what I'm here for.

Done right — no. Solar penetrations are flashed and sealed with materials rated for 25 years, exactly like any vent or skylight on your roof right now. If a leak from solar ever happens, it happens fast — usually within the first two years, from workmanship error — which is what the standard 10-year roof penetration warranty exists for.

Red flags worth knowing: repeated roof-leak complaints in an installer's reviews (one-offs happen; patterns don't lie), and paradoxically, a 25-year roof penetration warranty — that's a flashy sales gimmick from companies that won't exist long enough to honor it. And if your roof is old, replace it with the solar project: one crew coordination, one financing, and the roof portion can ride your program benefits. Roof + solar is one of the smartest combos in home improvement.

Deep breath — no. I've lived this from the inside (the company I worked with, KOTA, collapsed in 2024 despite doing right by customers). Here's what survives: all equipment warranties — panels (25-30yr), inverters (10-25yr), batteries (10-15yr) are held by manufacturers, not your installer. On a PPA or lease, the agreement itself is an asset that gets bought by another provider, warranties intact — you might not even notice the change beyond a new logo on the bill.

What you lose is the workmanship warranty — the free labor. Manufacturers will replace failed parts but may charge for the truck roll. Thousands of San Diego homes are "orphaned" like this right now, and it's exactly why I built my audit and service business: someone has to take care of these systems for the next 20 years. If that's you, reach out — you're not stranded.

An audit answers the question most solar owners can't: "Is this thing actually working for me?" Six data points tell the whole story: year-over-year consumption, year-over-year production, install date, current utility rates, your solar payment, and any true-up costs.

From those, the math falls out: yearly consumption × current utility rate = what you'd pay without solar. Subtract your annualized solar payments and true-ups = your real savings. Compare production to consumption = whether you're over- or under-producing, and whether usage changes (new EV? teenager with a gaming rig?) have outgrown your design. My audit also reviews your agreement terms against what's fair and flags rebates you're leaving unclaimed — SDCP funds, VPP enrollment, the works. If you've had solar 2+ years and never checked, you're overdue.

The true-up is your annual settle-up with the utility for energy your system didn't cover. Surprise bills almost always trace to one cause: your usage grew past your design. New EV, hot tub, home office, kids getting older, more AC in a hotter summer — the system was sized for the life you had, not the life you have.

Sometimes it's the design's fault (sized to a single bill instead of real usage data), and occasionally it's underproduction from a failed component. An audit separates the causes in about an hour: production data tells us if the system is healthy; consumption data tells us if your life outgrew it. The fix ranges from mode changes and habit shifts (free) to added panels or battery (an investment that beats paying the utility forever). What you shouldn't do is just quietly pay it every year.

In San Diego's low-rain climate, once a year is right — ideally before summer and after pollen season. The efficiency gain from cleaning is modest; the real benefit is keeping oxidizers and organic growth from degrading the glass over decades. Think of it like washing a car: skip it forever and the paint pays.

DIY is fine: de-ionized water, regular soap, soft brush, and never when panels are hot (thermal shock cracks glass). If your roof is steep or two stories, hire a cleaner — no efficiency gain is worth a ladder fall. Bundling cleaning with a periodic system inspection is the smart move; you're paying for the trip anyway.

Recycling & The Real Environmental Story

For the naysayers and the dreamers both — the honest accounting of what solar takes from the planet and what it gives back.

Yes — about 95% of a panel's materials are recyclable: silicon, aluminum, silver, copper, and glass, all recoverable and rollable into new products. The leading US recycler, SOLARCYCLE, now recovers up to 97% of a panel's value with a zero-waste process, and recycled aluminum takes 95% less energy than smelting new.

The honest caveat: America currently recycles only ~10% of decommissioned panels, because landfilling costs $1-5 per panel while recycling runs $15-45. That gap is closing fast (more below), and it's precisely the problem I'm building my recycling business to attack. Every panel that goes to a landfill is silicon, silver, and aluminum we mine twice for no reason.

This is moving faster than almost anyone realizes. SOLARCYCLE launched in 2022 charging $18/panel including shipping; today they run six US facilities (Georgia, Illinois, Texas, Nevada, Arizona), recycled over one million panels in 2025, are ramping to two million in 2026, and their new Georgia plant will scale to 5 gigawatts of panels per year. They're even breaking ground on a 1.1-million-square-foot facility making recycled solar glass for American panel manufacturing — panels made from panels. The circle, closing.

The economics are the last hurdle: recycling a panel costs ~$20 and yields $10-12 in recovered materials, so subsidies or regulation still bridge the gap. That's changing too — the EPA is finalizing "universal waste" rules for panels (final rule expected 2027), which will streamline recycling nationally. With 4-5 million US panels retiring yearly now and 10+ million projected by 2030, the companies building this infrastructure today are building the next energy supply chain. I intend to be part of it.

Lithium-ion batteries are recyclable — and it matters doubly, for the environment and because the materials inside (lithium, cobalt, nickel) are exactly what the next generation of batteries needs. Redwood Materials leads the US effort, recovering up to 95% of critical metals from used batteries, with innovations like solar-powered processing making the loop cleaner still.

We're earlier on the curve than panels — home battery recycling infrastructure is still developing, and the EPA is working on streamlined rules. But the timeline works in our favor: batteries installed today have 10-15 years before end-of-life, which is exactly the runway the recycling industry needs to scale. Improper disposal risks fires and wastes irreplaceable materials, so when your battery's day comes, it goes to a recycler. Period.

Is the manufacturing process clean? Absolutely not — mining, refining, and fabrication all carry real impacts, and pretending otherwise insults your intelligence. Can we make it cleaner? Most definitely, and domestic manufacturing under stricter US environmental standards is doing exactly that.

But here's the accounting that matters: a panel pays back its manufacturing energy in 1-3 years, then produces clean power for another 25+. It's a one-time cost with a 25-year dividend — and at end of life, 95% of it is recoverable. Fossil fuels invert every part of that: fuels that took millennia to form, destroyed instantly with every burn, polluting continuously, recyclable never. When was the last time you heard of an innovation in oil & gas beyond "drill baby drill"? Solar's imperfections are engineering problems on a solving trajectory. Combustion's imperfections are physics.

Solar serves two purposes for our civilization right now: local power independence — the grid is old, costs are exploding, and ratepayers deserve a predictable alternative — and what I call delayed energy generation recycling. Every solar + battery installation starts a clock: 25-30 years for panels, 10-15 for batteries, to build the recycling chains that turn them into the next generation of themselves.

Fossil fuels give us no such clock — every burn is final. Solar, wind, hydro, and geothermal extend our timeline to develop even better answers: longer batteries, more powerful panels, small modular nuclear. I work in this industry because it thinks forward. The old way burns something and it's gone. The new way creates possibility. That's the whole belief system, honestly.
